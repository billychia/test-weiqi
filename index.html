📜 History of Weiqi (围棋)

Origin: Weiqi (called "Go" in the West) originated in China over 4,000 years ago, making it one of the oldest board games still played today. Legend credits the mythical Emperor Yao (尧) with creating the game to educate his son.

In Ancient China: Weiqi was a pastime of scholars, nobles, and military strategists. It was considered an essential skill for the educated elite. The game was played on a 17x17 board until the 19x19 standard emerged during the Tang Dynasty (618–907 AD).

🏯 The Four Arts of China (四艺):

Weiqi was one of the Four Arts that every gentleman scholar was expected to master:

  • 琴 (Qín) — Music (guqin playing)
  • 棋 (Qí) — Board game (Weiqi)
  • 书 (Shū) — Calligraphy
  • 画 (Huà) — Painting

These arts represented the ideal of a well-rounded, cultured individual.

Spread to Japan & Korea: Weiqi reached Japan in the 7th century, where it became known as Go (碁). It flourished under the patronage of shoguns and samurai. The Japanese established professional houses and refined the game's theory. In Korea, it is called Baduk (바둑) and became deeply embedded in Korean culture, with many top modern players hailing from Korea.

South East Asia: Weiqi spread through trade routes, with smaller but dedicated communities in Vietnam, Thailand, Singapore, and Malaysia. It remains a niche but growing activity.

The West & Africa: Go arrived in Europe in the 19th century via Japanese diplomats. Today, Europe has a strong Go scene with national federations, tournaments, and even professional players. In the USA, the American Go Association (AGA) promotes the game. Africa has small but passionate communities, particularly in South Africa and Nigeria, with growth supported by online platforms.

Modern Era: The 21st century has seen a revolution — AI systems like AlphaGo (2016) defeated top human players, sparking global interest and changing how the game is studied and played.

🎯 Objective: Enclose More Territory

The goal of Weiqi is to control more territory than your opponent. Territory is the empty intersections surrounded by your stones.

♟️ Contrast with Chess:

In Chinese Chess (象棋) and International Chess, the objective is to capture the King (or General) — a destructive goal. In Weiqi, the goal is constructive: you build and surround territory, not eliminate the opponent's leader.

"Weiqi is a game of balance and influence, not annihilation."

📏 Basic Rules

1.4.1 — Two Players, Black Moves First

Black places a stone on any empty intersection, then White, then Black, and so on until both players pass.

⚫ Black's turn — click to place a stone

⬆️ Try placing stones! Click on intersections. Black moves first, then White alternates.

1.4.2 — Stones on Empty Intersections, No Moving

Stones are placed on intersections of the grid lines (not in the squares). Once placed, a stone cannot be moved — it stays until captured (if ever).

✅ Try it above — you can only place on empty intersections.

1.4.3 — Liberties & Capturing

A liberty is an empty intersection directly adjacent (up, down, left, right) to a stone. A stone with no liberties is captured and removed.

1.4.4 — Connected Stones Share Liberties

Stones of the same color that are adjacent (up/down/left/right) are connected. They share liberties and live or die together.

1.5

🙏 Etiquette & Manners

Weiqi is a game of respect and mindfulness. Here are key etiquette points:

🙇 Bow before starting

Show respect to your opponent and the game.

🤫 Silence & concentration

Avoid distractions. Think before you move.

✋ Place stones gently

Clicking stones loudly is considered impolite in traditional settings.

⏳ Don't stall

Play at a reasonable pace. Respect your opponent's time.

🤝 Resign with dignity

If you're losing, place two stones on the board to resign, then thank your opponent.

📖 Review together

After the game, it's customary to review key moves with your opponent.

🧘 The Spirit of Weiqi:

"Win without arrogance, lose without resentment." The game is a mirror of your character — patience, humility, and clarity of mind are as important as tactical skill.
